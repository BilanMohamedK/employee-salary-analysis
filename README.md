# Employee Salary Analysis Project

## Project Overview
This project analyzes employee salary patterns and demographic factors to understand how compensation varies across departments, roles, and age groups.

The goal is to identify trends in salary distribution and workforce structure using SQL for data analysis and Excel for visualization.

---

## Tools Used
- SQL (data extraction and analysis)
- Excel (data visualization and dashboard creation)

---

## Business Questions
- How does salary vary across departments?
- Do certain occupations earn significantly more than others?
- Is salary related to age or demographic factors?
- Who are the highest-paid employees?

---

## SQL Process
1. Explored raw datasets (employee demographics and salary tables)
2. Joined datasets using employee_id to create a complete employee profile
3. Calculated average salary by department, occupation, and age
4. Identified top 5 highest-paid employees

---

## Key Findings

### Department Salary Differences
Department 3 and 6 have the highest average salaries, indicating more consistently high-paying roles.
Department 1, despite having several top earners, has a lower overall average (~56k), suggesting a wider salary distribution with both high-earning and lower-paying roles.
This suggests that average salary alone may not fully capture compensation structure, and additional metrics such as salary distribution or median salary would provide deeper insight.

---

### Salary by Occupation
There is a strong variation in pay across occupations. City Manager is the highest-paid role (90,000), followed by senior administrative and parks leadership roles. Lower-paid roles such as assistants, musicians, and shoeshiners fall between 20,000–25,000, indicating a wide occupational pay gap.

### Salary and Age Trends
Salary generally increases with age, peaking in mid-career (late 30s to early 40s). However, the trend is not strictly linear, suggesting that job role and department have a stronger influence on salary than age alone.

---

### Top Earners
The highest-paid employees are concentrated in Departments 1, 3, and 6, showing certain departments contain more senior or high-value roles.

---

## Overall Conclusion
This analysis shows that salary differences are primarily driven by job role and department rather than demographic factors such as age. 
Departments with higher average salaries tend to have more senior or specialized roles, while others show a wider distribution of pay, indicating a mix of high-earning and lower-paying positions within the same department. 
Additionally, while salary generally increases with age, the relationship is not consistent, suggesting that role and organizational structure have a greater impact on compensation than experience alone.
Overall, the findings highlight the importance of considering both role type and departmental structure when analyzing salary patterns, as averages alone may not fully capture variations within the workforce.
